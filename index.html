7 — What it means to be a private midwife

What does it mean to be a private practice midwife?

The International Confederation of Midwives — Definition of a Midwife
https://internationalmidwives.org/resources/international-definition-of-the-midwife/

The ICM defines a midwife as a person who has successfully completed a midwifery education programme based on the ICM Essential Competencies for Midwifery Practice, acquired the requisite qualifications to be registered and/or legally licensed to practice midwifery, and who demonstrates competency in the scope of practice of the midwife.

"The midwife is recognised as a responsible and accountable professional, who works in partnership with women to give the necessary support, care and advice during pregnancy, labour and the postpartum period, to conduct births on the midwife's own responsibility and to provide care for the newborn and the infant. This care includes preventative measures, the promotion of normal birth, the detection of complications in mother and child, the accessing of medical care or other appropriate assistance and the carrying out of emergency measures."

The midwife has an important task in health counselling and education, not only for the women and gender diverse people they serve, but also within families and communities. This work should involve antenatal education and preparation for parenthood and may extend to sexual and reproductive health care, and care for infants and young children.

A midwife may practise in any setting including the home, community, hospital, clinic or health unit.

As you can see, working as a private midwife is not a rogue or outlandish choice to make. According to the definition of a midwife, you are working according to the definition and scope of a midwife. Remember, your choice to be in private practice is endorsed by the International Confederation of Midwives. You are a responsible, capable and autonomous care provider and registered health care professional. Remind yourself and other people of this if your choice to work in private practice is challenged. In an ideal world, working to your full scope in midwifery should be celebrated and not discouraged.

Here in Australia the title and skill of midwives is protected by law, this means that only midwives who are registered can claim to be or use midwifery skills. From this perspective, unregistered midwives cannot call themselves midwives, you have a midwifery degree, but you are not a midwife in Australia unless you are registered.

Registration allows for regulation of the midwifery profession. I encourage you to stay completely above reproach in this regard. I encourage you to see regulation as a positive thing for our profession because it ensures that a minimum standard and expectation is maintained (in theory!). Regulation means that women know what to expect from midwives and don't inadvertently hire someone who is not held to a standard and who has no professional training.

The first and most important thing to do as a private midwife is to ensure that you have ticked all the boxes regarding your regulation requirements according to The Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency and the Nursing and Midwifery Board — we cover these in detail in the next section.

7b — Two things to remember as a private midwife

Two things to remember as a private midwife

Two of the first things we have to realise about what it means to be a private midwife is that not only are you a registered and regulated health professional with clinical and registry responsibilities, you are also in business. These are 2 completely separate things and each need ongoing attention and professional development. You also have some additional requirements in alignment with your registration that other midwives might not need to consider.

1. You're in business

Being in business is a skill set in itself and this brings new responsibilities and regulation in your role as a midwife. This includes how you conduct yourself on social media, on your website and in your marketing efforts. Midwives must adhere to our regulatory requirements regarding marketing and social media use as we conduct business.

2. You are an autonomous practitioner with no employer supervising you

This means that you must self regulate. There is no intermediary process between you and AHPRA or the NMBA. It is your responsibility to self-regulate and be prepared to defend and prove this if required. If you were employed, this regulation would be instituted and ensured by your employer, but you are self employed now so you must build compliance strategies into your business and clinical work flows. Google Workspace — Introduction

Setting up your Business Administration

Google Workspace

Google Workspace is the professional, business-grade version of Google tools such as Gmail and Google Drive. It is a cloud-based suite of productivity tools — including custom email, shared calendars, and secure document storage — designed to help you run a professional practice from your laptop or smartphone.

In Australia, health information is considered "sensitive information." This means you have a higher duty of care under the Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles (APPs). Google Workspace provides the infrastructure to help you meet your obligations for the secure storage and handling of patient records.

Key benefits for your practice
  • Secure Client Information: Use Google Drive to store antenatal records, blood results, and birth plans with strict "View Only" or "Editor" permissions.
  • Professional Identity: An email like care@yourname.com.au signals to hospitals, GPs, and Medicare that you are a registered, professional healthcare provider.
  • Mobile Clinical Notes: Access your files securely on any device.
  • Seamless Intake with Google Forms: Send clients a digital intake form — health histories and consent forms feed directly into a private spreadsheet.
  • Telehealth Ready: Google Meet offers secure, encrypted video calling easy for clients to use without downloading extra software. You can enable Gemini notes for a transcript and summary of the appointment.
  • Business tools: Your Workspace account makes it easy to set up Google Ads, Google Business, Google Analytics, Slides, and more.

11b — Building your presence (back-end)

Building your Presence in the Birth Industry

Build the back-end of your business

The back end of a business is all the things no one would ever or should ever see. This is all the stuff that you have to do, just to operate as a business. Very few things in your business will be what we call 'front facing', (the things that people see and experience from you and your business). The back-end is where most of the sweat and hard work is. The back end of a business is the internal, "behind-the-scenes" infrastructure that supports operations, administration, and fulfilment, enabling the front-end (client-facing) activities to function.

Start building your back-end before you start providing a service. Back-end activities include:

  • Setting up your business structure — sole trader, trust, company — and all the paper work that goes into making this a legitimate and above board structure
  • Completing endorsement
  • Getting your Medicare and PBS provider number
  • Maintaining your registration
  • Deciding and registering a business name
  • Brand and design
  • Getting a domain name and building the website
  • Starting a Social Media Page
  • Finding an accountant — learning how to use that accountant!
  • Setting up and learning accounting software
  • Opening a Business bank account
  • Gathering supplies and skills
  • Understanding business at all!
  • Administration tasks and invoicing
  • Standard operating procedures — eg: how will you bill clients and receive payments, what does your care contract include and when will they sign it? how will you store it? do you have a system to maintain confidentiality in your client notes
  • Establishing a solid clinical documentation process — are you going to purchase a digital notes platform? which one?

These things represent just an example of some of the things that happen at the back-end of any business. You can start building the back-end before providing any front end services. In fact, it is essential that you have all of your back end structures in place before starting to take clients. It's not lost time — you can start building, creating and learning about the back end of business years before ever providing a service.

11c — Building your presence (social media value)

What is the Value in a Social Media Account?

What is the value in a social media account?

One way people can really get to know you and hopefully ultimately decide to hire you, is by putting yourself out there on social media. Again, start slow and make your account private if you like while you find your feet. Your client base, women between 20-40 are on social media and they are your target clients.

There is so much to learn about being on social media and how to present yourself online. I would suggest:

  • Creating an account dedicated to business and keeping your personal account private and personal. Conducting yourself professionally online is essential for your registration but also keeps your personal and professional life separate.
  • Being generous with the content you post on social media — always think about what value or information you can give to your audience. This will build a grateful, interested and invested audience who could potentially become your clients later down the track.
  • Being genuine — there is no need to perform, take your clothes off or become someone new. Just be who you are and you will attract the audience who appreciates you and will act as an introduction to potential clients.
  • Make the name of your social media account as close to your business name and website address as possible to help people find you and present a cohesive brand.

15 — Authoritative knowledge

Authoritative Knowledge

Authoritative knowledge is defined as the knowledge that participants agree counts in a particular situation, and it's the basis on which they make decisions and provide justifications for their course of action. Although essentially subjective and not necessarily correct, its power is that it counts.

In a medicalised context, ownership of the artefacts and equipment used to manage labour displays who possesses the authoritative knowledge. When this rests with the medical establishment, the birthing woman turns to her caregivers for information and defers to their expert knowledge. The mother's reliance upon medical knowledge to determine her baby's wellbeing overshadows her own intuitive ability to know her baby.

Unlike in clinical settings, in a social birthing context like a homebirth or freebirth, the woman and her attendants are considered the authorities and the woman's intuitive ability to know is given authority and legitimacy.

Between the 1920s and 1970s, western medicine enjoyed a golden age of dominance over pregnancy and childbirth. From the 1970s onwards, there was an increase in awareness of the social, emotional and spiritual dimensions of health and a corresponding erosion of trust in medical authority. This cultural shift has since grown into the consumer birth movement, which advocates for natural birth methods and challenges the need for a doctor at every birth.

15b — Authoritative knowledge (continued)

Authoritative Knowledge

The current risk discourse about birth which is perpetuated by the medical culture, dictates that giving birth is risky and hospitals are the safest place to give birth. But Andrew Bissits (2016) suggests that the risks associated with pregnancy and birth are low prevalence phenomena, however there is an obstetric tendency towards an emphasis on risk. He suggests that when presented with the range of ways pregnancy and birth can go wrong, women begin to feel that pregnancy is indeed dangerous.

Perpetuating fear by promoting risk is an effective strategy to position doctors as the saviours from the perceived dangers of childbirth; it allows the birthing community to exist in a heightened sense of alarm and thus increases their willingness to attend hospitals (Dahlen 2016). Scamell calls this the 'scare factor' (Scamell 2016) and, De Vries (1993) argues medical professionals gain control by inventing risk.

The 'medical gaze' was most prominently recognised in the work of Foucault, where certain conditions or behaviours are perceived through this medical gaze and then Physicians, by labelling and viewing something as pathological, can legitimately lay claim to all activities concerning the condition. Under the medical gaze, certain cohorts within society are designated as either compliant or deviant, exhibiting good or bad behaviour.

From a Foucauldian perspective, conformity to the medically defined norm is seen as good behaviour. The decision to birth outside the system or challenge the current system can be understood as a way of escaping the medical gaze and the women who do this are consciously or subconsciously labelled as deviant.

In the paradigm proposed by Foucault, escaping the medical gaze would categorise these women as social deviants. Nibbs (2011) argues that compliance with authoritative knowledge produces the good citizen, and therefore the repudiation of this knowledge designates citizens as deviant.

Cohain (2012) states that 'the belief that hospital birth for low-risk pregnancies has better outcomes than planned, attended homebirth is an urban legend. The choice of low-risk women to deliver in hospital is a result of the dominant and irrational human propensities to gossip, to follow the crowd and to cling to irrational hope'. These viewpoints challenge and subvert western culture's authoritative knowledge about birth. The decision to shun medical management of birth constitutes a counter-cultural approach to decision-making.

Just because maternity based authoritative knowledge has social and cultural authority, this does not mean it's right or true.

About GST and BAS

At this stage you don't have to apply for GST or fill out a BAS statement. Once you start earning over $75,000 per year you will need to apply for GST and do a quarterly BAS statement. However — midwives don't charge GST for their services, we are GST exempt. But we do pay GST in our businesses.

If you register for BAS before you start earning over $75,000 per year you have the advantage of being able to claim back any GST that you paid through the year for your business. It's up to you to balance the cost of doing your BAS (yourself or via an accountant) against how much GST you might be able to claim back.

24b — Paying your second midwife (pay split)

How to Pay your Second Midwife — The Pay Split Method

The pay split method — how Mel pays the second midwife

In order to pay the second midwife I use a pay split method. Using this method there is a 'time sheet' in every woman's notes and the midwife records the time and date of each appointment including the number of minutes of care that were given. All points of care are recorded here except phone calls.

This time sheet acts as a care record for Medicare and also in case care is terminated early (so previous care can be accounted for and billed). After the woman is discharged, the primary midwife calculates the pay split and pays the second midwife based on the number of minutes of care provided. In this example:

  • This woman was a repeat client so I charged her $6,500 for her care
  • $500 is allocated to the booking midwife before the pay split is completed on the remainder
  • In this circumstance $6,000 was split between the two midwives according to the number of minutes of care provided
The pay split formula
  • Determine how many minutes of care were given in total by adding all the minutes together — in the example: 1,410 minutes total
  • Allocate $500 to the primary midwife before splitting the rest — in the example: $6,000 remaining
  • Divide the remaining amount by the number of minutes — in the example: $6,000 ÷ 1,410 = $4.25/minute
  • Calculate how many minutes of care each midwife gave — in the example: second midwife gave 285 minutes of birth care
  • Multiply the number of minutes by the minutely rate — in the example: 285 × $4.25 = $1,212.70

Using the pay split method each midwife gets paid for the actual time of care given. Some births are faster or longer and some women require lots of hours of care (translating to a lower minutely rate for all the midwives) and some women need fewer hours of care (resulting in a high rate of pay per minute). If all of the midwives are using the pay split system, you start to realise that the ups and downs come out in the wash as you attend more and more births together.

A special note about money and your work-wives

The thing that is most likely to undo your work relationship is money. I suggest being very clear with financial expectations and procedures from the very beginning. Have it written down, have a clear standard operating procedure and never stray from it. Have a working contract that stipulates the terms of your arrangement and use this as the standard process. We are all here working in business as our JOB and that means fair pay for the fair work completed. Maintain a high standard of work ethic toward each other and your clients, always have each other's back and always pay the right amount and always on time. This keeps working relationships strong.

Step 13 — Grey area / early antenatal care planning

The Grey Area — Between Booking and First Appointment

The Grey area between booking and first appointment

As soon as you have formally booked a woman into your care you should hurry to identify her needs so you can put preventative measures in place. If you are her sole care provider and she is not seeing a GP, don't delay her first appointment. Starting care between 5-7 weeks is important in this circumstance. Now that the collaborative agreement is no longer in place, midwives can provide Medicare services without a referring doctor. This means that women don't need to see a GP for their initial antenatal care, a midwife can fill this role.

In order to provide comprehensive care in early pregnancy, midwives should consciously up-skill. Although this type of antenatal care is within our scope, we have not had a chance to fully explore this.

This early care is also about flagging potential risk factors that could be prevented or managed early to prevent future complications. For example:

  • A client whose mum had pre-eclampsia in her pregnancies, this puts my client at risk. I have recommended calcium and magnesium supplementation and also fish oil/Essential fatty acid supplementation for her pregnancy as a preventative measure and offered her the opportunity at Nuchal Translucency and Triple blood Screening for the purpose of understanding placentation. We have also discussed antenatal aspirin.
  • A client of Japanese heritage who confesses to eating a high sugar diet — she declined formal glucose tolerance testing, but given her risk factors, I offered HbA1c blood test in antenatal screen and BSL testing. Her Blood sugar levels were high and out of range for the full week of testing so I provided her with diet education and a glucometer and advised BSL testing for the remainder of the pregnancy. We managed this without consultation or referral because her BSL has been effectively managed with diet.
  • A client with antiphospholipid syndrome who has had 3 previous pregnancy losses and also 3 of her previous babies with me at home. She is pregnant again and I have recommended immediate early viability scan and an obstetric consult soon after to consider management options including the possibility of aspirin and/or clexane.

These are all women who I am caring for and who are planning homebirths, but who will have very different pregnancy outcomes with early planning and education.

35 — Consultation and referral

Consultation & Referral — The ACM Guidelines

Consultation and referral planning — 'they' are going to want to see your 'risk assessment'.

The ACM guidelines are meant as a tool to help prompt you to scenarios that may require you to consult with someone (or the woman consults) or refer the woman on to an additional care provider. The guidelines are NOT a risk assessment tool, however many people assume they are and use them as such. A midwife can care for a woman regardless of which category she falls into A, B or C, however, some category items require additional care team members. The guidelines are supposed to guide you as to which scenarios require consultation and/or referral.

Technically, if the woman accepts consultation and referral and follows your advice to do this, she is not 'outside the guidelines' — she is technically complying with the guidelines by accepting consultation or referral. However, I always write a record of understanding if a woman is a category B or C, even if she accepts consultation and referral. This is so I can defend my practice if an incident should occur and a health practitioner decides to make a formal complaint.

If the woman has complicating factors requiring you to ask for care from other health care providers, you will also open yourself up to the possibility of being reported to AHPRA by these health professionals. What we know is that reports are most likely to be lodged against us by other health professionals and not by the woman herself.

35b — Discussing risk and safety

Discussing Risk and Safety

We have to remember that 'risk' and 'safety' are actually subjective constructs. They are made up and fabricated opinions about how safe or dangerous a particular circumstance is. It is a medical tool to use 'risk' and 'safety' as the guiding factors in making decisions about what women should and shouldn't submit to for their care.

My suggestion is when caring for women who are outside of the guidelines, let go of the words 'risk' and 'safety'. Without the option of those words, its time to start discussing the best way to share information with women so she can proceed with making decisions about her care.

In order to properly educate and inform a woman about her choices she doesn't need to be told about the 'risk' of something happening to her and her baby, she gets to decide if something is risky or safe, not you. If the woman is the one who decides what is safe and what poses a risk to her and her baby, its your job to help her access the information that will help her calculate and determine the risk and safety of her decisions.

36 — Managing labour and birth

Managing Labour and Birth

What do homebirth midwives do at birth? This is one of the biggest questions that women ask and also midwives… many midwives and women don't know what the alternative is to constant monitoring and observations of the labouring woman… what is the alternative, what do midwives do when there isn't much to do? I'll talk you through what I do during an uncomplicated homebirth.

Most often birth care starts remotely over the phone when a woman first notifies you that something is changing and she might be in labour. This is the time for you to start making a few arrangements to ensure you are available and ready — prepare your car and your birth kit, notify the second midwife, make plans for childcare if you need to, you may need to shuffle a few appointments in anticipation and you might consider not going too far from home if the woman has a history of quick births.

During antenatal care I've already been speaking to the woman about early labour management and so hopefully they are prepared to manage the early stages of labour as a family unit and with their other support people. I always encourage families to have another support person in addition to the woman's partner because they will both need support through the labour and birth process for things like childcare, food, resting, household management and caring for pets. This is also important in terms of transfer planning. If families don't have social support, encourage them to hire a doula. This support network is where early labour care starts and it's only when they have stretched their capacity that the care of a midwife is warranted.

Early labour management includes encouraging the woman to deny that she is in labour as long as she can, prioritise resting, eating and drinking and preparing her birth space. I always speak with women about how long this early labour phase can go for and remind them that early labour is still labour and so resting between contractions is what is going to give them longevity in the labour and birth process. I encourage my clients to utilise heat packs in early stages of labour and employ their calmbirth or hypnobirth techniques.

In these early hours of labour there is not much midwifery care that women need, it's more about supportive care by phone and answering questions that the woman has.

I remind my clients that they don't need to send updates in the night because I'm always ready to come if they need me. I explicitly state that if they don't need me overnight it's better that I sleep and am well-rested to attend them in later labour.

Managing Labour and Birth (continued)

Once the woman phones you and asks you to attend it's time to go. The second midwife is already arranged and I put out a set of clothes so I can dress quickly. The car always has at least half a tank of petrol so you don't need to fill up on the way to or from the birth. I take a personal care bag that has water, snacks, tea making supplies, extra socks and jumpers to keep warm and a change of clothes. I also pack my phone charger and a small crochet project in case the birth goes on for a while. I always inform the woman of how long it will be to arrive and to call again if something is changing so that I can alert the second midwife if the birth is close.

Upon arrival I bring in all of my equipment and position it out of the sight of the woman and not in the birth space that she has set up. I also keep it out of the reach of children and pets. Before setting everything up I quietly let the woman know I'm there and ask if she needs anything.

The only initial check that I do is on foetal heart rate before setting up my equipment. While I'm setting up I make some notes in the woman's folder about the run-up to my arrival and also take note of the spacing and intensity of her contractions based on her sounds and behaviour. Depending on the woman's unique needs I may attend maternal obs soon after setting up if she is in a capacity to allow these. I try and find times that she is already in a state of distraction or transitioning through her birth space to interfere with a midwifery task.

You will know what specific care the woman needs in labour because you have been her care provider throughout her antenatal care. Don't underestimate the power of having known the woman prior to labour and how much this impacts upon the type of care that you give. Continuity of care is a major factor in tailoring birth care to the individual woman.

If the woman is well and healthy, often the only clinical task is checking foetal heart rate with a doppler every 15-30 minutes… and remember, if the woman is having an extended rest, the baby is not under stress — contraction pattern should dictate foetal heart rate monitoring from my experience.

Many other tasks take your time at birth such as reheating the pool, filling up the woman's drink, helping her to the bathroom, maintaining the temperature of the room, heating up heat packs, massaging, tidying up the birth space, having tea and snacks, attending to the needs of the siblings and partner, having a little sleep for yourself and documenting the events of the labour and birth.

The only clinical care performed is that which is specific to the needs of the woman, nothing, except FHR monitoring and perhaps BP, pulse and temperature (if indicated) should be done routinely.

When conducting yourself at birth remain as quiet as possible, ask as few questions as possible, when approaching the woman, come low and in her eye sight rather than standing over her. This maintains an appropriate power dynamic.

Managing Labour and Birth (continued)

When it comes to the actual birth, hands on or hands off makes no difference to perineal outcomes but if the woman is out of the water I do offer a warm pack to the perineum and split the arms in to the body if it's a compound presentation. The important thing with supporting the birth is to gently and quietly offer verbal support to let her know that everything is safe and normal and feedback what is happening.

Remind the woman to slowly and gradually move their baby out. At home we don't do coached pushing unless the baby is in distress and needs to be born quickly, otherwise we just sit back and allow the woman to tap into what her body is already telling her what to do.

As the baby emerges either the woman or the partner can receive the baby or the midwife can pass the baby through to the parents so they can collect the baby to the mother's chest.

Once the baby is out and immediately skin to skin, Apgars are attended, a towel offered to keep the baby warm and then the midwife keeps completely quiet and out of the way. It's not the time to congratulate the parents, ask them what the sex of the baby is or ANYTHING unnecessary… it's now time for you to move out of the space and make a cup of tea.

Keep the room and pool warm, watch for blood loss and neonatal well-being at a distance. This is now the time for the woman and baby to be flooded with oxytocin, to meet each other — the parents will be feeling incredible joy, excitement and love. 37 — Managing clinical urgency

Managing Clinical Urgency at Birth

Firstly, let's remember that clinical urgency does not mean you are having an emergency.

For example, if you notice that a woman is losing blood, you are experiencing a time of clinical urgency and it's time to act. If you give syntocinon, the placenta is born completely and the woman stops bleeding, you have managed a situation of urgency before having an emergency.

If this same woman continues to lose blood and becomes symptomatic of a postpartum haemorrhage, this is the time she requires emergency management; which you as a trained midwife are capable of providing.

The second thing to remember is that most urgent clinical situations are either preventable or predictable. So before you are in an emergency situation, trust your instinct and skill to detect and act upon possible urgent situations. My go-to rule for preventing emergencies is NOT to wait and see what happens if you have noticed things are not tracking normally and the woman or baby is becoming compromised.

If a labour or birth are deviating from the normal physiological process then it is time to consider transfer to hospital. Homebirth is best and safest for normal, uncomplicated labour and birth — where abnormality starts to develop, that's when we enlist the help of a hospital service.

41 — Keeping yourself safe

Keeping yourself Safe in Private Practice

"IF IT'S NOT DOCUMENTED IT DIDN'T HAPPEN. Provide excellent care, operate your business diligently, and document everything — in more detail than you think."

Justifying clinical care

Our only option then in private practice is to rely on:

  • Our personal scope of practice
  • The woman's particular preferences
  • The woman's clinical picture
  • Our own clinical reasoning
  • What the evidence says about 'best practice'
  • The Australian College of Midwives guidelines for consultation and referral
  • Existing Public Policy documents (if you would like a standardised approach)

These are the things that dictate our clinical practice. 41b — Committing to a sustainable workload

Committing to a Sustainable Workload

Committing to a sustainable workload is the number one key to keeping you emotionally, socially, psychologically, professionally and physically safe in private practice and protecting you from burn out. If you are in your 20s, 30s or 40s and this is the model you want to work in for the rest of your career — you've got 30–40 years of work life ahead of you, and you need to protect yourself so you can sustain that.

Working out how much time you have

A useful exercise: list out the things that use your time in order of priority (don't include work), then plot these on a weekly planner including sleep time. The gaps represent the time you have to commit to working.

Understanding how much time each client takes

Depending on which model of care you choose to work in, this will look different, but for me, I assume that one woman will take 40 hours to care for over the course of her care, which is one total week. So a full work week for me is 1 client. Therefore, a full-time workload is 4 clients per month. If I want to have a quiet month and only work part-time I'll take fewer clients.

Now that you know how much time you have, see if you can have a go at working out how much time each of your clients will take to look after. Once you know how much time each client will need from you and how much time you have actually got, then you can work out how many clients you can fit in without compromising the time you have for your other life commitments.

41c — Committing to a sustainable workload (continued)

Committing to a Sustainable Workload (continued)

You might find yourself in a situation now, after doing this little exercise, where you realise that you don't actually have enough work time to maintain the income that you need to sustain your lifestyle… so now you need to ask yourself a harder question — am I willing to adjust my lifestyle to match my sustainable income? This is where the work is, and lifestyle adjustments are what take the most time and effort.

You might find that in order to fit your life to the time you have to work you might need to sell and move house, budget, cut out some purchases… or readjust your time commitment to fit in more work so you can maintain the lifestyle you have chosen… the risk here is that you will over work and set up a life model that doesn't sustain your emotional, social, psychological, professional and physical health, or the health of your family.

Personally, we have always only based our lifestyle and expenses off ONE person's income, when we bought our house we only borrowed the amount that ONE person could pay off. This meant that at any time one person could sustain the lifestyle we had built and we would never put ourselves in a circumstance that required both of us to work. Over the years, both of us have worked, but that just means extra money to pay off the mortgage quicker or spend money on the garden or other important things. We have chosen to live simply, take small cheap holidays instead of big expensive ones, live a minimalist lifestyle and only spend money on things that we deem important or beautiful.

Living this way has allowed us to experiment with different family employment configurations, take up study/university degrees, homeschool our children, work part-time and never feel financially stretched.

I'm just using my life as an example here — I'm not saying I've got it right. We have just done it this way because this supports our main priority which is to have enough time to homeschool our children, prioritise family time and commit to our friend and church life while also growing our own food and eating well and indulging in all our other interests.

The final step is to use self discipline to see when you have reached your working capacity and consciously say no to any other work tasks that seek to steal your time from other important things. If you are already booked for the month don't take any more clients. You'll lose a client and some money but you will maintain your life health and family health and all the other facilities of health... then you'll be well enough to continue working for years and years in a sustainable work structure.

There will be times to stray from your alloted work time and periodically you will be busier than you anticipated, but if this is not habitual, you and your family will recover from these unexpected interruptions.

Setting realistic client expectations is a way of setting boundaries around the service you are willing to provide. One possibility in private practice is that your clients expect a level of care you never intended to give — then if you don't meet their expectations, they get disappointed.

This is a text I received recently at 6:30am, it woke me up:

41d — Keeping yourself safe: records & HCCC

Keeping Yourself Safe

"If it's not documented, it didn't happen."

The main way to keep yourself safe is to provide excellent care and document it in detail. Documentation also relates to your CPD records, business management and finances. Leave a paper trail for everything — self-explanatory enough to stand without you there to explain it.

Justifying clinical care

In private practice, clinical decisions are guided by: your personal scope of practice, the woman's preferences and clinical picture, your own clinical reasoning, the evidence base, ACM guidelines for consultation and referral, and existing public policy documents.

When documenting clinical management, record these factors explicitly. If your care is ever called into question, you can demonstrate you thought critically and systematically — that is all anyone can ask.

41e — Sustainable workload: time per client

Sustainable Workload: Time Per Client

One client takes approximately 40 hours of your time from booking to discharge — the equivalent of one full working week. A full-time workload is roughly 4 clients per month. Adjust to suit your life.

Working out your capacity
  1. List everything that uses your time, in priority order — exclude work for now.
  2. Plot these on a weekly planner including sleep. The gaps are your available work time.
  3. Calculate how many client hours fit in those gaps without compromising your priorities.
  4. Use that number to set your maximum caseload per month.
The harder question

If there isn't enough work time to maintain the income you need, you face a choice: adjust your lifestyle to match a sustainable income, or adjust life commitments to create more work time. Neither is wrong — but doing this consciously is what protects you from burnout.

Self-discipline to say no

Once you know your capacity, the final step is using self-discipline to say no when you've reached your limit. You'll lose a client and some money — but you'll maintain your health, your family and your longevity in private practice.

42e — Holiday planning & practice hacks

Holiday Planning & Practice Hacks

Planning holidays and clinical breaks

Part of maintaining your longevity in private practice is to consciously plan holidays and clinical breaks ahead of time — otherwise it won't happen. Choose when you want a break, then decline anyone due in that window. Leave a 2–3 week buffer on each side, or arrange for your second midwife to hold primary responsibility for any clients who birth during your time off.

Mel's approach: alternate one Christmas on, one Christmas off. Only take clients due early December or late January in the off year. You will never regret taking a break — but you will regret not planning one.

Hacks to make private practice easier
  • Sync your watch with your phone — always have an accurate time record for notes.
  • Keep your car packed and ready at all times during on-call periods.
  • Enter client contacts with full detail: name, address, EDD, partner's number.
  • Save important contacts in your phone: hospitals, back-up midwives, pathology labs.
  • Develop a 'leaving for a birth' rhythm so you never forget anything.
  • Always pack a phone charger in your birth bag.
  • Keep your car at least half full with petrol throughout your on-call period.

35d — Consultation & referral: the guidelines explained

Consultation & Referral: The Guidelines Explained

The ACM guidelines for consultation and referral are a tool to help prompt you to scenarios that may require consultation or referral — they are not a risk assessment tool, even though many people use them that way.

A midwife can care for a woman regardless of which category (A, B or C) she falls into. Some category items require additional care team members — but the guidelines are designed to guide, not to exclude women from your care.

When you consult or refer
  • Document the consultation or referral clearly in your contemporaneous notes.
  • Use a 'Record of Understanding' template when a woman declines a recommendation.
  • Templates are available on the online mentorship platform under 'Record of Understanding'.
  • The woman always retains the right to decline — document this thoroughly every time.
The medicolegal conversation

Other care providers — GPs, obstetricians, hospital staff — will often want to see your 'risk assessment'. Understanding that the guidelines are a consultation prompt, not a risk exclusion tool, allows you to engage with this conversation with confidence and clinical authority.
