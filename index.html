7 — What it means to be a private midwife

What does it mean to be a private practice midwife?

The International Confederation of Midwives — Definition of a Midwife
https://internationalmidwives.org/resources/international-definition-of-the-midwife/

The ICM defines a midwife as a person who has successfully completed a midwifery education programme based on the ICM Essential Competencies for Midwifery Practice, acquired the requisite qualifications to be registered and/or legally licensed to practice midwifery, and who demonstrates competency in the scope of practice of the midwife.

"The midwife is recognised as a responsible and accountable professional, who works in partnership with women to give the necessary support, care and advice during pregnancy, labour and the postpartum period, to conduct births on the midwife's own responsibility and to provide care for the newborn and the infant."

A midwife may practise in any setting including the home, community, hospital, clinic or health unit. Working as a private midwife is not a rogue or outlandish choice — you are working according to the full definition and scope of a midwife, endorsed by the International Confederation of Midwives.

Here in Australia the title and skill of midwives is protected by law — only registered midwives can claim to be or use midwifery skills. I encourage you to stay completely above reproach and to see regulation as a positive thing for our profession.

7b — Two things to remember as a private midwife

Two things to remember as a private midwife

1. You're in business

Being in business is a skill set in itself and brings new responsibilities and regulation in your role as a midwife. This includes how you conduct yourself on social media, on your website and in your marketing efforts. Midwives must adhere to our regulatory requirements regarding marketing and social media use as we conduct business.

2. You are an autonomous practitioner with no employer supervising you

This means you must self-regulate. There is no intermediary process between you and AHPRA or the NMBA. It is your responsibility to self-regulate and be prepared to defend and prove this if required. Some strategies to put in place include:

  • Regularly scheduling a time to do Midwifery Practice Review — you could start one of your own, do the ACM MPR, or join the Maintenance Program and complete MPR each year with your peers.
  • Plan for yearly emergency skills training
  • Make a Continuing Professional Development plan to ensure you have your hours
  • Automate your insurance payments so this never lapses
  • Put money aside to commit to paid training or conferences

Google Workspace — Introduction

Setting up your Business Administration

Google Workspace

Google Workspace is the professional, business-grade version of Google tools such as Gmail and Google Drive. It is a cloud-based suite of productivity tools — including custom email, shared calendars, and secure document storage — designed to help you run a professional practice from your laptop or smartphone.

In Australia, health information is considered "sensitive information." This means you have a higher duty of care under the Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles (APPs). Google Workspace provides the infrastructure to help you meet your obligations for the secure storage and handling of patient records.

Key benefits for your practice
  • Secure Client Information: Use Google Drive to store antenatal records, blood results, and birth plans with strict "View Only" or "Editor" permissions.
  • Professional Identity: An email like [email protected] signals to hospitals, GPs, and Medicare that you are a registered, professional healthcare provider.
  • Mobile Clinical Notes: Access your files securely on any device.
  • Seamless Intake with Google Forms: Send clients a digital intake form — health histories and consent forms feed directly into a private spreadsheet.
  • Telehealth Ready: Google Meet offers secure, encrypted video calling easy for clients to use without downloading extra software. You can enable Gemini notes for a transcript and summary of the appointment.
  • Business tools: Your Workspace account makes it easy to set up Google Ads, Google Business, Google Analytics, Slides, and more.

10b — Gather skills and qualifications

Gather Skills and Qualifications

Before starting your private midwifery business, it is important to fully understand your own scope and skill set. Once you understand it, you know your next steps to plug any skill or scope holes and where to direct your professional development efforts.

Some skills that are helpful for private practice
  • Perineal suturing, sterile water injections, cannulation, administering IV fluids (without a machine), urinary catheterisation, taking blood samples
  • Newborn screening tests, using a Pinards, vaginal examinations, assisting vaginal breech birth
  • Shoulder dystocia management and neonatal resuscitation skills
  • Using a TENS machine
  • Physiological management of placental birth and converting to active management for PPH care
  • Understanding alternatives to common interventions — iron infusions, perineal repair without sutures, determining labour progress without VE, strategies for turning malpositioned babies
  • Develop a set of pharmacology script templates for your reference

If you cannot acquire a skill that you feel is essential, consider another option — for example, when choosing your second midwifery colleagues for particular births, select the ones who have the skill you lack. As you set up your clinical practice, use your scope and skill to select clients and colleagues carefully.

11c — Building your presence (community)

Building your Presence in the Birth Industry

Start attending or hosting gatherings

The homebirth community loves a get together! Finding and attending these gatherings in your area — or starting one if they don't yet exist — is a guaranteed way to keep your finger on the pulse of the birth culture in your area and allow people to get to know you and choose you as their midwife.

Get to know the key players

I'm being completely honest here — don't assume that private midwives or birth workers will be nice to you; many midwives will see you as competition. There are a few incredible midwives who are kind and generous — those are the ones you need to connect with. Also, be kind and generous yourself. A midwife buddy or 'work-wife' within proximity is your greatest strategy for thriving in private practice.

Do some research online, notice the people at gatherings you attend, identify experienced and successful birth workers with a positive reputation. A great way to find your tribe is to be of service to other people — make yourself available as an emergency on-call back-up for local midwives, or offer something of value like a template you've created.

Start offering some services

If you are also a lactation consultant, childbirth educator, calmbirth practitioner or similar, consider starting a 'slow burn side-hustle' while you build your big business. Starting and running a business is largely the same regardless of the service — every time you engage in business, you learn more about business.

15 — Authoritative knowledge

Authoritative Knowledge

Authoritative knowledge is defined as the knowledge that participants agree counts in a particular situation, and it's the basis on which they make decisions and provide justifications for their course of action. Although essentially subjective and not necessarily correct, its power is that it counts.

In a medicalised context, ownership of the artefacts and equipment used to manage labour displays who possesses the authoritative knowledge. When this rests with the medical establishment, the birthing woman turns to her caregivers for information and defers to their expert knowledge. The mother's reliance upon medical knowledge to determine her baby's wellbeing overshadows her own intuitive ability to know her baby.

Unlike in clinical settings, in a social birthing context like a homebirth or freebirth, the woman and her attendants are considered the authorities and the woman's intuitive ability to know is given authority and legitimacy.

Between the 1920s and 1970s, western medicine enjoyed a golden age of dominance over pregnancy and childbirth. From the 1970s onwards, there was an increase in awareness of the social, emotional and spiritual dimensions of health and a corresponding erosion of trust in medical authority. This cultural shift has since grown into the consumer birth movement, which advocates for natural birth methods and challenges the need for a doctor at every birth.

"The belief that hospital birth for low-risk pregnancies has better outcomes than planned, attended homebirth is an urban legend." — Cohain (2012)

© Melanie the Midwife 2026

15b — Authoritative knowledge (continued)

Authoritative Knowledge

Perpetuating fear by promoting risk is an effective strategy to position doctors as the saviours from the perceived dangers of childbirth. Scamell calls this the 'scare factor' and De Vries argues medical professionals gain control by inventing risk.

The 'medical gaze', most prominently recognised in the work of Foucault, allows physicians — by labelling something as pathological — to legitimately lay claim to all activities concerning the condition. The decision to birth outside the system can be understood as a way of escaping the medical gaze, with women who do this consciously or subconsciously labelled as deviant. Conformity to medically defined norms produces the 'good citizen'; repudiation of this knowledge designates citizens as deviant.

Just because authoritative knowledge has social and cultural authority, this does not mean it is right or true. As we go on through this mentorship we will ensure that your business processes, documentation and clinical approach all align to the legislative requirements for private midwives and that compliance is built into your standard operating procedures.

20b — About GST and BAS

15 Steps to Starting your Business

About GST and BAS

At this stage you don't have to apply for GST or fill out a BAS statement. Once you start earning over $75,000 per year you will need to apply for GST and do a quarterly BAS statement. However — midwives don't charge GST for their services, we are GST exempt. But we do pay GST in our businesses.

If you register for BAS before you start earning over $75,000 per year you have the advantage of being able to claim back any GST that you paid through the year for your business. It's up to you to balance the cost of doing your BAS (yourself or via an accountant) against how much GST you might be able to claim back.

Although midwives don't charge women GST, payments made between midwives — for example, paying your second midwife — are supposed to include GST.

24 — Paying your second midwife

How to Pay your Second Midwife

There are several arrangements midwives use: a flat fee for birth attendance ($600–$1500), bartering birth attendance, or the second midwife invoicing the woman directly. The thing most likely to undo a working relationship is money — be very clear with financial expectations and procedures from the beginning, have it written down, and never stray from it.

The pay split method — how Mel pays the second midwife

A 'time sheet' in every woman's notes records the time, date and number of minutes of care given. After the woman is discharged, the primary midwife calculates the pay split based on minutes of care provided.

The pay split formula
  • Add all minutes of care together — example: 1,410 minutes total
  • Allocate $500 to the primary midwife before splitting the remainder
  • Divide remaining amount by total minutes — example: $6,000 ÷ 1,410 = $4.25/min
  • Calculate each midwife's minutes — example: second midwife attended 285 minutes
  • Multiply minutes by rate — example: 285 × $4.25 = $1,212.70

I advise against business partnerships — you can agree to work together but not have the back-end of your business reliant on another person.

Step 13 — Grey area / early antenatal care planning

The Grey Area — Between Booking and First Appointment

As soon as you have formally booked a woman into your care, hurry to identify her needs so you can put preventative measures in place. 35 — Consultation and referral

Consultation & Referral — The ACM Guidelines

The ACM Guidelines are a tool to prompt you to scenarios that may require consultation or referral. They are NOT a risk assessment tool. A midwife can care for a woman regardless of which category she falls into — A, B or C. Technically, if the woman accepts consultation and referral, she is complying with the guidelines.

"Always write a Record of Understanding if a woman is a category B or C, even if she accepts consultation and referral. This protects your practice if an incident occurs."

Up to 50% of private midwives have had a complaint lodged about them — most of which are completely vexatious. It is in your best interest to always keep documentation that will serve to protect you if you ever have to answer a complaint. What we know is that reports are most likely to be lodged by other health professionals, not by the woman herself.

35b — Discussing risk and safety

Discussing Risk and Safety

'Risk' and 'safety' are actually subjective constructs. When caring for women who are outside of the guidelines, let go of those words and start thinking about the best way to share information so a woman can proceed with making her own decisions. It is her job to decide if something is risky or safe — your job is to help her access the information that will help her make that calculation.

"Providing education does not sound like 'there is a risk of uterine rupture.' Providing information sounds like: 'A caesarean section leaves a scar on your uterus — this is thought to lead to uterine rupture in 0.7% of women. This means 99.3% of women will not experience a rupture.'"

Here no mention of death, danger, risk or safety has been made — just information. Now it's up to the woman to decide if this is a risk she is willing to take. We take risk every day — we get in our cars, walk across the street, enter into relationships. Everything we do poses some danger to us, but we decide if that risk is worth the benefit.

The best book to read about the conceptualisation of risk and how obstetrics has hijacked risk jargon as a tool to frighten women is Jo Murphy Lawless' Reading Birth and Death: a history of obstetric thinking — essential reading for anyone working in maternity care.

36 — Managing labour and birth

Managing Labour and Birth

Most often birth care starts remotely by phone. Prepare your car and birth kit, notify the second midwife, and make childcare plans. Encourage families to have another support person in addition to the partner — this is also important for transfer planning. I remind clients that they don't need to send updates in the night — if they don't need me overnight, it's better that I sleep and am well-rested to attend them in later labour.

If the woman is well and healthy, often the only clinical task is checking foetal heart rate with a doppler every 15–30 minutes. When conducting yourself at birth remain as quiet as possible, ask as few questions as possible, and when approaching the woman come low and in her eye sight rather than standing over her.

"Stay out of the way and only insert yourself in the birth space if the woman invites you or has a genuine clinical need. Stay quiet and unobtrusive."

When to call your second midwife
  • When you think the woman is entering the pushing stage
  • If the woman previously had quick births — call early
  • If you are unsure about how to manage the birth
  • If you have been at the birth a long time and require rest
  • Multiparous clients — consider calling earlier

37 — Managing clinical urgency

Managing Clinical Urgency at Birth

Clinical urgency does not mean you are having an emergency. Most urgent situations are either preventable or predictable. Trust your instinct and skill to detect and act upon possible urgent situations early. My go-to rule: do NOT wait and see if you have noticed things are not tracking normally.

Most likely situations of clinical urgency
  • Breech birth
  • Concerning foetal heart rate
  • Shoulder dystocia
  • Postpartum haemorrhage
  • Maternal collapse
  • Baby requiring resuscitation after birth
Top tips for managing urgency
  • Trust your instinct and midwifery skills — we are capable!
  • Keep physiology and the umbilical cord intact — only interrupt physiology if absolutely necessary
  • Don't wait and see; there is no shame in transferring to hospital

41b — Committing to a sustainable workload

Committing to a Sustainable Workload

Committing to a sustainable workload is the number one key to keeping you emotionally, socially, psychologically, professionally and physically safe in private practice and protecting you from burn out. If you are in your 20s, 30s or 40s and this is the model you want to work in for the rest of your career — you've got 30–40 years of work life ahead of you, and you need to protect yourself so you can sustain that.

Working out how much time you have

A useful exercise: list out the things that use your time in order of priority (don't include work), then plot these on a weekly planner including sleep time. The gaps represent the time you have to commit to working.

Understanding how much time each client takes

For Mel, one woman takes approximately 40 hours of care over the course of her pregnancy — one total work week. So a full-time workload is 4 clients per month. Once you know how much time each client will need and how much time you have, you can work out how many clients you can fit in without compromising the rest of your life.

Activity
Plot every responsibility on a weekly planner, including sleep. The gaps represent your available work time. Now plan the amount of work that fits there — and don't take any more.

42 — Professional relationships and self-care

Professional Relationships & Self-care

Nurturing professional relationships

Most of the time in private practice, our need for positive working relationships with other health care providers is greater than their need for us. Give your private practice midwifery colleagues the time and energy you would want to receive back. Prioritise answering their calls, responding to their texts, and providing emergency back-up if they get stuck. Most of the time, being responsive and supportive will establish a culture of reciprocity.

Your professional relationships with midwives who work within a hospital have also been incredibly valuable — do your best not to burn bridges with these midwives and identify particular midwives who will be most valuable to your work as a private midwife.

Using your support network

Do an audit of who is in your support network — you are going to need them. Reliable midwifery back-up has been the single most important factor in Mel's personal survival in private practice. Do your utmost to be a reliable back-up midwife and to secure one for yourself.

Plan regular holidays

Consciously plan holidays, slow times and clinical breaks into your year ahead of time. Choose when you want your break and say no to anyone due in that time. You'll never regret taking a holiday, but you will regret not planning one.

Hacks to make private practice easier
  • Sync your watch with your phone
  • Always have your car packed and ready
  • Enter client contacts in your phone with detail
  • Always pack a phone charger; keep car at least half full with petrol
  • Have a 'leaving the house for a birth' rhythm so you don't forget anything

Setting realistic client expectations

Setting Realistic Client Expectations

Setting realistic client expectations is a way of setting boundaries around the service you are willing to provide. One possibility in private practice is that your clients expect a level of care you never intended to give — then if you don't meet their expectations, they get disappointed.

Some examples of Mel's clear expectations with clients:

  • If it is not an urgent situation, please do not contact your midwife outside of business hours. Call or text anytime if you are worried about you or your baby's health.
  • Any complications that require round-the-clock care or periodic daily checks are out of the scope of this service. If you develop a complication that needs close and frequent clinical management, we may need to escalate care to a hospital.

"In my responses I always make it about me rather than accusing the woman of anything — for example: 'For non-urgent questions, can you try and reserve them for business hours? I have my phone close to me at night and I like to sleep in, so early morning texts wake me.'"

Activity
What expectations will you set clearly with your clients from the outset? Write out your key boundaries and how you will communicate them.
